Data Visualization: Inequality of COVID-19 Vaccinations

September 5, 2021

As developed countries head for booster shots, a rising concern on the inequality of COVID-19 vaccines distributions is coming to surface. Is this concern, however, exaggerated?

Percentage of People Fully Vaccinated

Source: Our World in Data

Well… It’s not. As Portugal nears 75% of full vaccination rates on its population, there are still countries not reaching 1%. Perhaps unsurprisingly, African countries rate among the worse, as only 3% of the continent’s population is fully vaccinated against COVID-19.

Issues of morality

The World Health Organization has already caught up on this issue, with its head Tedros Ghebreyesus stating that it’s unacceptable that countries which have used most of the global supply of vaccines keep using even more of it. Hence, by beginning of August, a halt was recommended on vaccine boosters, to allow at least 10% of the population of every country to be fully vaccinated.

We are, nevertheless, far from reaching that goal.

But the issue goes beyond morality

When countries have small proportions of the population vaccinated against COVID-19, the risk of a sustained community spread increases. This means that the virus has room to mutate into more aggressive variants, which will likely be able to infect vaccinated people. Thus, the low vaccination rates on poor countries also impacts richer countries.

The path forward

Despite the difficulties, the goal is simple: to vaccinate the population of the poorer countries against COVID-19. The estimated value to reach this goal is around $50 billion, according to the WHO. And what is $50 billion exactly? As Christine Lagarde would put it, only 1% of what was spent in fiscal supports by developed countries due to the current crisis.

So, all that is needed in order to reach a great leap in the fight against COVID-19 is a bit more generosity from richer countries towards the poorest.
